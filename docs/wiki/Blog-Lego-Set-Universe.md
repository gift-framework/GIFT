# What if the universe was a Lego set we received in loose pieces?

Apr 22, 2026

---

*We may have received the universe as one enormous Lego set in bulk: no box, no picture on the lid, no instructions. And what if physics were, deep down, nothing more than a long collective attempt to rebuild the missing manual?*

---

When you open a large Lego set, there are two ways of looking at it.

Either as a heap of coloured plastic. Or as something already structured, whose logic simply escapes us for now.

The pieces are there. The final model exists in potential, somewhere, in the way they are going to fit together. But without the instructions, we don't yet know what any of it means. We sort, we fumble, we assemble, we take apart, we start over. Little by little we begin to recognise families of pieces, recurring gestures of assembly, zones that seem destined to go together. And slowly, something takes shape.

I often feel that science looks a lot like this.

---

We didn't make the pieces. We find them already there. We try to understand what they are, which ones are fundamental, which ones serve to connect, which ones only make sense in combination with others. And above all, we try to understand what kind of whole they make possible.

Physics, seen from this angle, doesn't begin by inventing the pieces. It tries to recognise their logic of assembly.

## The simple bricks

Some pieces are immediate. Sturdy, indivisible at their scale. A 2×2 brick. A 4×2. You can count them. You can stack them. They give structure.

You recognise them at a glance, you know what they're for: building a wall, filling a volume, laying a base. These are the pieces of *how many*.

In mathematics, they are called *discrete invariants*: quantities you can count, and that change the very nature of an object. A number of holes. A number of independent cycles. A whole-numbered dimension. You can't have "two point three" fundamental holes in an object. At that level, reality speaks in whole numbers.

## The specialised pieces

But a Lego set doesn't only contain generic bricks.

It also contains stranger pieces. A hinge. A wheel. An axle. A clip. A flag. These aren't just "extra pieces". They're pieces that open up assembly possibilities no standard brick allows. You don't swap a hinge for a 2×2. You don't swap a flag for a ball joint. Each one does something the others simply cannot.

They are rare, irreducible, and play a role that is disproportionate to their number in the overall architecture. Mathematicians call *irreducible structures* this kind of piece: the atoms of an assembly, what no longer factorises. The **prime numbers** are one example.

## The theme of the set

And then there is something subtler still.

Two Lego sets can contain more or less the same pieces, and yet tell completely different stories. One will be a city. Another a medieval castle. Another a spaceship.

The *theme* imposes general lines, silhouettes, ratios of scale. A castle will have round towers and arches. A spaceship will have sleek fuselages, taut curves. It isn't the pieces that change, it's the **recurring forms** that organise them.

Physics has this level too. Beyond the whole numbers that count, beyond the rare pieces that articulate, there are **forms** that show up everywhere. Wherever there's a rotation, a cycle, a wave, the same signature returns: π, the constant of the circle. Wherever there's a growth that feeds on itself: a savings account earning interest on its own interest, a population reproducing, a wave propagating, it's *e*, Euler's constant.

These numbers don't count anything. They don't articulate anything. They set the style. Their decimals never end, because they encode a shape that no ratio of whole numbers can ever capture exactly. Mathematicians call them *irrational*.

The integers give you the frame. The special pieces allow articulation. The continuous forms give the theme. And the assembly manual I'm proposing seems to use these mathematical constants to tune nature itself.

## G.I.F.T. as a candidate manual

This is probably the simplest way I can describe GIFT (Geometric Information Field Theory).

Not as yet another theory that adds more pieces to the pile. But as an attempt to reconstruct part of the missing instructions.

Take the constant called α, the "fine-structure constant". It's the volume knob of the universe for electromagnetism. Turn it up by 4%, and stars stop making carbon, and therefore so do we. Turn it down by 4%, and atomic nuclei collapse. Its value, 1/137.036, has been measured with dizzying precision. But no one has ever known why it takes *that* value, and not another.

Another example: there are exactly *three* families of matter in the universe. Three versions of the electron, each heavier than the last. Three versions of every quark. Not two. Not five. Three. Nobody knows why.

GIFT poses a simple question: what if these numbers weren't arbitrary? What if they were the **consequences of a mode of assembly**? The bricks were there, the special pieces were there, the theme was there. The manual articulated them in a certain way. We read the finished products today without yet knowing how to read them backwards. GIFT tries to read them.

Physicists have been studying the pieces for a very long time, with admirable power. GIFT asks a slightly different question: *what if the real secret isn't only in the pieces, but in the way they fit together?*

What I'm proposing, concretely, is a manual that uses more than 99% of the available pieces, without leaving any on the side of the table, without adding any that weren't in the box. And when you follow that manual to the end, the model that comes out looks *almost perfectly* like the one we've always seen in the display case: the world, as we measure it. Not exactly. Almost. But close enough to suggest there's a thread worth pulling, and far enough from perfection to know there's still work to do.

## The stories we tell ourselves

There's one last detail, and it's the one that moves me most.

When a child opens a Lego set and starts playing, they almost always invent a story. The character is called this, they live there, they're looking for that. The story wasn't in the box. It isn't on the instructions. It emerges from the pieces as they meet; probably endlessly, probably a little irrationally, and yet never chaotic. It has its thread, its logic, its inner coherence.

I think our physical theories are a bit like these stories.

The pieces, the manual, the theme: all of that is *already there*, in nature, for fourteen billion years. But our *theories*, the ones we build to explain all of this, are our stories. They are not reality itself. They are mathematically constrained narratives, built to follow as closely as possible the way the pieces really seem to fit together.

GIFT is one more story. Perhaps a slightly unusual one, because it tries less to invent a plot than to read the manual, but a story all the same, with its limits, its edges, its shadowed corners.

## We're all doing the same thing

Perhaps we've all been doing the same thing for centuries. Sorting pieces, comparing patterns, testing assemblies, correcting our mistakes, starting over.
