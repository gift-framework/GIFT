\begin{abstract}
We compute the Kaluza--Klein spectrum and harmonic cohomology of a compact twisted
connected sum (TCS) 7-manifold $\Kseven$ with Betti numbers $(b_2, b_3) = (21, 77)$, using
the explicit 169-parameter $\Gtwo$ metric constructed in our companion paper~[I]. All
computations exploit the adiabatic decomposition $\Kseven \approx K3 \times T^2 \times I$ along the TCS
neck, which we validate numerically to $< 0.002\%$ fiber variation across 5
independent tests.

The main results are: (i)~what appears to be the first explicit numerical
computation of scalar, 1-form and 2-form Kaluza--Klein spectra on a compact $\Gtwo$
manifold, with spectral gap $\lambda_1 = 0.1244$ and Weyl exponent $\alpha = 1.998$; (ii)~spectral
confirmation of all Betti numbers $b_0 = 1$, $b_1 = 0$, $b_2 = 21$, $b_3 = 77$; (iii)~explicit
construction of 21 harmonic 2-forms and 77 harmonic 3-forms with radial profiles,
revealing a $2210\times$ self-dual/anti-self-dual gap in the K3 intersection matrix;
(iv)~spectral democracy: the scalar, 1-form, and 2-form Laplacians share identical
spectra to $10^{-4}$ precision, a consequence of the near-constant fiber metric;
(v)~stability of the spectral gap under singular perturbations of the K3 fiber.

Connections between these spectral data and quantities arising in particle physics
are noted in Appendix~E and developed systematically in the author's companion
framework (see Author's Related Work).

All scripts and results are provided as a companion notebook ($< 5$ minutes total
runtime, CPU-only) for independent verification.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Context and Motivation}

The spectral geometry of compact Riemannian manifolds with special holonomy is a
classical subject with rich connections to topology, representation theory, and
mathematical physics~\cite{ref13,ref14,ref15}. While the existence of compact 7-manifolds with
holonomy $\Gtwo$ was established by Joyce~\cite{ref5,ref6}, and the twisted connected sum (TCS)
construction of Kovalev~\cite{ref7} and Corti--Haskins--Nordstr\"om--Pacini~\cite{ref8} has produced
large families of examples, essentially no numerical spectral data has been computed
on any compact $\Gtwo$ manifold: the existence theorems do not produce pointwise
numerical values of $g_{ij}(x)$.

In our companion paper~[I], we constructed an explicit approximate metric on a
compact TCS 7-manifold with $(b_2, b_3) = (21, 77)$, given in closed form as a
Chebyshev--Cholesky expansion with 169 parameters. A computational Newton--Kantorovich
certificate establishes the existence of a unique torsion-free $\Gtwo$ metric within
relative distance $4.86 \times 10^{-6}$ of our iterate.

This paper exploits the explicit metric to compute the spectral geometry of $\Kseven$:
Laplacian eigenvalues and harmonic forms across all relevant form degrees. The
adiabatic structure of the TCS neck reduces all 7D spectral problems to families of
1D Sturm--Liouville ODEs, making comprehensive numerical computation tractable.
Specifically, we compute:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The Kaluza--Klein spectrum (scalar, 1-form, 2-form Laplacians)
\item All harmonic forms (confirming the Betti numbers spectrally)
\item The self-dual/anti-self-dual structure of the K3 intersection form
\item Spectral democracy across form degrees
\item Stability of the spectral data under singular deformations
\end{enumerate}

We are not aware of comparable numerical spectral data on a compact $\Gtwo$
manifold in the existing literature, though we do not claim an exhaustive
survey. Substantial numerical work exists for \textit{non-compact} $\Gtwo$
manifolds (see e.g.~\cite{ref9,ref10}), but compactness is essential for the
Laplacian spectrum to be discrete.

\subsection{The Adiabatic Ansatz}

The TCS structure of $\Kseven$ provides a natural dimensional reduction. The manifold
decomposes as
\begin{equation}
\Kseven = (M_1 \times S^1) \;\cup_{K3 \times T^2 \times I}\; (M_2 \times S^1)
\end{equation}
where $M_1$, $M_2$ are asymptotically cylindrical Calabi--Yau threefolds, and the neck
region is diffeomorphic to $K3 \times T^2 \times I$ with $I = [0, 1]$. In the neck, the metric
takes the block-diagonal form
\begin{equation}
ds^2 = g_{ss}(s)\, ds^2 + g_{\theta\theta}(s)\, d\theta^2 + g_{\psi\psi}(s)\, d\psi^2 + g_{K3}(s)
\end{equation}
where $s \in [-2, 3]$ is the seam coordinate, $(\theta, \psi)$ parametrize the $T^2$ fiber, and
$g_{K3}(s)$ is a $4 \times 4$ metric on K3. This $s$-dependent block structure reduces
7-dimensional PDEs to families of 1-dimensional Sturm--Liouville ODEs, one for each
fiber mode.

The adiabatic ansatz is \textit{not} an approximation imposed a priori: it is a consequence
of the TCS geometry, which we verify a posteriori to high precision. Five independent
tests confirm that, for the present smooth metric, the fiber is numerically
constant along the neck to high precision:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
Test & Observable & Measured & Expected \\
\midrule
Fiber flatness & max $s$-variation of $g_{K3}(s)$ eigenvalues & $< 0.002\%$ & 0 \\
Adiabatic additivity & Error in $\lambda_{n,(m,q)} = \lambda_n^{(0,0)} + V_{(m,q)}$ & 0.003--0.023\% & 0 \\
Weyl law & Eigenvalue growth exponent $\alpha$ & 1.998 & 2.0 \\
$T^2$ isotropy & Splitting $|g^{\theta\theta} - g^{\psi\psi}|$ & $3 \times 10^{-7}$ & 0 \\
K3 near-roundness & Spread of K3 metric eigenvalues & $< 0.1\%$ & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Five independent tests of the adiabatic decomposition.}
\label{tab:adiabatic_tests}
\end{table}

These tests provide strong numerical evidence that the adiabatic decomposition is
an excellent approximation for the present smooth metric. Whether this level of
accuracy persists for singular $\Kseven$ geometries or for quantities sensitive to
fiber-dependent modes remains to be investigated.

\section{The Metric}

This section summarizes the metric construction of~[I]; the reader is referred there
for full details. We fix notation and record the key properties needed for the
spectral analysis.

\subsection{Chebyshev--Cholesky Construction}

The metric $g_{ij}(s)$ on $\Kseven$ is parametrized as follows. At each point $s$ along the
seam coordinate, the $7 \times 7$ metric tensor is reconstructed from a lower-triangular
Cholesky factor $L(s)$ with entries given by Chebyshev polynomial expansions of order
$K = 5$. With $7 \times 7 = 49$ independent entries (28 from the lower triangle), each
expanded in 6 Chebyshev coefficients, plus normalizations and constraints, the total
parameter count is 168 for the Chebyshev sector plus 1 for the asymptotically
cylindrical ($\ACyl$) decay rate $\gamma = 5.81$, yielding 169 parameters.

The metric is \textbf{symmetric positive definite (SPD) by construction}: the Cholesky
factor guarantees $g = LL^\top > 0$ everywhere. The determinant is fixed algebraically
to $\det(g) = 65/32$. The norm of the associative 3-form satisfies $|\varphi|^2 = 42$.

\section{Scalar Laplacian Spectrum}

\subsection{Method: Adiabatic Sturm--Liouville}

On $\Kseven$ with the adiabatic metric $g(s)$, a scalar eigenfunction $f$ of the
Laplace--Beltrami operator $\Delta_0$ decomposes as
\begin{equation}
f(s, \theta, \psi, x_{K3}) = e(s) \times \exp[i(m\theta + n\psi)] \times \eta(x_{K3})
\end{equation}
where $(m, n) \in \mathbb{Z}^2$ labels the $T^2$ Fourier mode and $\eta$ is an eigenfunction of the K3
Laplacian with eigenvalue $\mu$. For each channel $(m, n, \mu)$, the radial function $e(s)$
satisfies the Sturm--Liouville problem
\begin{equation}\label{eq:SL}
-\frac{d}{ds}\left[p(s) \frac{de}{ds}\right] + q_{m,n,\mu}(s)\, e(s) = \lambda\, w(s)\, e(s)
\end{equation}
with coefficient functions determined by the metric:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Coefficient & Definition & Physical meaning \\
\midrule
$p(s)$ & $\sqrt{\det(g)} \times g^{ss}$ & Radial diffusion \\
$w(s)$ & $\sqrt{\det(g)}$ & Volume weight \\
$q(s)$ & $\sqrt{\det(g)} \times [m^2 g^{\theta\theta} + 2mn\, g^{\theta\psi} + n^2 g^{\psi\psi} + \mu\, g^{K3}]$ & Fiber potential \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Sturm--Liouville coefficient functions.}
\label{tab:SL_coefficients}
\end{table}

\textbf{Discretization.} We use a uniform grid with $N = 800$ points on $s \in [-2, 3]$,
yielding step size $h = 6.26 \times 10^{-3}$. The SL operator is discretized as a symmetric
tridiagonal matrix, and the generalized eigenvalue problem is solved using
\texttt{scipy.linalg.eigh\_\allowbreak tridiagonal}. Convergence is verified by Richardson extrapolation
from 6 grid sizes ($N = 200, 400, 600, 800, 1000, 1200$).

\textbf{Boundary conditions.} For the $(0, 0, 0)$ channel, we use both:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Dirichlet}: $e(-2) = e(3) = 0$ (justified by exponential tail decay
  $e^{-2\gamma \times 2} \approx 10^{-10}$).
\item \textbf{Neumann}: $de/ds = 0$ at $s = -2$ and $s = 3$ (captures the zero mode).
\end{itemize}
The comparison resolves the spectral gap unambiguously (\S\ref{sec:gap}).

\subsection{Spectral Gap and Weyl Law}\label{sec:gap}

\textbf{Zero mode.} The Neumann computation yields a lowest eigenvalue
$\lambda_0 = 3.47 \times 10^{-13}$, consistent with machine-precision zero. The corresponding
eigenfunction is constant to within numerical noise. This confirms $\mathbf{b_0 = 1}$
spectrally: $\Kseven$ is connected.

\textbf{Spectral gap.} The first nonzero eigenvalue is
\begin{equation}
\lambda_1 = 0.1244 \pm 0.0001
\end{equation}
converged via Richardson extrapolation:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{cc}
\toprule
$N$ & $\lambda_1$ (Neumann) \\
\midrule
200 & 0.12346 \\
400 & 0.12409 \\
600 & 0.12430 \\
800 & 0.12440 \\
1000 & 0.12446 \\
1200 & 0.12450 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Convergence of the spectral gap with grid refinement.}
\label{tab:convergence}
\end{table}

The Dirichlet computation gives $\lambda_1 = 0.1247$, confirming that the first Dirichlet
eigenvalue \textit{is} the spectral gap (not a Dirichlet artifact of the zero mode).

\textbf{Kaluza--Klein scale.} The spectral gap determines the KK mass scale:
\begin{equation}
m_{\KK} = \sqrt{\lambda_1} / L = 0.353 / L
\end{equation}
where $L$ is the characteristic length of the seam coordinate. Setting $m_{\KK} = M_{\GUT}$
determines $L$.

\textbf{Weyl law.} For a Riemannian manifold of dimension $d$, Weyl's asymptotic law
predicts $\lambda_n \sim C n^{2/d}$ as $n \to \infty$. Our 1D effective problem should give
$\lambda_n \sim C n^2$ ($\alpha = 2$). Fitting the first 20 eigenvalues yields
\begin{equation}
\alpha = 1.998, \quad C = 0.125
\end{equation}
The near-perfect agreement with $\alpha = 2$ independently validates the metric
construction and the adiabatic reduction.

\subsection{$T^2$ Channels and Adiabatic Additivity}

The $T^2$ Fourier decomposition predicts that higher channels $(m, n) \neq (0, 0)$ should
have eigenvalues shifted by a constant potential:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{k,(m,n)} = \lambda_k^{(0,0)} + V_{(m,n)}
\end{equation}
where $V_{(m,n)} = m^2 g^{\theta\theta} + 2mn\, g^{\theta\psi} + n^2 g^{\psi\psi}$. This additivity is a
consequence of the fiber metric being independent of $s$.

\textbf{9 channels computed} (60 eigenvalues each, 540 total):

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
Channel $(m,n)$ & $\lambda_1$ & $V_{(m,n)}$ & Theoretical $V$ \\
\midrule
$(0,0)$ & 0.1247 & 0 & 0 \\
$(1,0)$ & 0.9801 & 0.855 & $g^{\theta\theta} = 0.855$ \\
$(0,1)$ & 0.9801 & 0.855 & $g^{\psi\psi} = 0.855$ \\
$(1,1)$ & 1.8356 & 1.711 & $g^{\theta\theta} + g^{\psi\psi} = 1.711$ \\
$(1,-1)$ & 1.8356 & 1.711 & $g^{\theta\theta} + g^{\psi\psi} = 1.711$ \\
$(2,0)$ & 3.5464 & 3.421 & $4\, g^{\theta\theta} = 3.421$ \\
$(0,2)$ & 3.5464 & 3.421 & $4\, g^{\psi\psi} = 3.421$ \\
$(2,1)$ & 4.4018 & 4.277 & $4\, g^{\theta\theta} + g^{\psi\psi} = 4.277$ \\
$(1,2)$ & 4.4018 & 4.277 & $g^{\theta\theta} + 4\, g^{\psi\psi} = 4.277$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{$T^2$ channel eigenvalues and adiabatic additivity verification.}
\label{tab:T2_channels}
\end{table}

The adiabatic additivity is exact to within 2\%, limited by the Dirichlet boundary
conditions, not by physical $s$-dependence of the fiber. Key observations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$T^2$ isotropy}: $(m, n)$ and $(n, m)$ channels are degenerate to $3 \times 10^{-7}$.
  This confirms $g^{\theta\theta} = g^{\psi\psi} = 0.855$ spectrally.
\item \textbf{Quadratic scaling}: $V_{(m,n)} = 0.855 \times (m^2 + n^2)$, a perfect $m^2 + n^2$ law.
  The $T^2$ is a rigid isotropic fiber.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{K3 Channels and the Full KK Tower}

The K3 fiber contributes additional eigenvalue channels through its own Laplacian
eigenvalues $\mu_{K3}$. These are treated analogously to the $T^2$ channels: each K3
eigenvalue $\mu$ adds a potential $V = \mu \times g^{K3}(s)$ to the Sturm--Liouville problem.

\textbf{K3 adiabatic additivity} is verified on 8 test channels (with K3 eigenvalues
ranging from $\mu_1$ to $\mu_8$), with gap errors of 0.003--0.023\%.

\textbf{Effective fiber metric.} The inverse metric on the 6D fiber $K3 \times T^2$ is
block-diagonal:
\begin{equation}
g^{\mathrm{fiber}} = \mathrm{diag}\big(\underbrace{0.855, 0.855}_{T^2},\; \underbrace{1.208, 1.208, 1.208, 1.208}_{K3}\big)
\end{equation}
The K3 block is nearly round: its eigenvalues are $[1.2075, 1.2087]$, with spread $< 0.1\%$.

\textbf{Three-scale hierarchy.} The first eigenvalue in each fiber sector determines a
mass scale:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}

\midrule
Neck (radial) & $\lambda_1 = 0.125$ & $\sqrt{\lambda_1} = 0.353$ \\
$T^2$ & $V_{(1,0)} = 0.855$ & $\sqrt{V} = 0.925$ \\
K3 & $V_{K3,1} = 1.208$ & $\sqrt{V} = 1.099$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Three-scale hierarchy from the fiber structure.}

\textbf{Complete KK tower.} Enumerating all states below $\lambda = 20$ using the additive
decomposition yields 1744 distinct eigenvalues (4460 states with multiplicities).

\section{Hodge Laplacian on 2-Forms}

\subsection{$b_2 = 21$ Spectral Confirmation}

The computation reveals a clear spectral structure:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{21 near-zero eigenvalues}: $\lambda < 10^{-8}$ for the first 21 modes.
\item \textbf{22nd eigenvalue}: $\lambda_{22} \approx 0.12$.
\item \textbf{Gap ratio}: $\lambda_{22}/\max(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_{21}) \approx \mathbf{14{,}635}$.
\end{itemize}

The gap ratio exceeds $10^4$, making the identification of $b_2 = 21$ unambiguous. The
21 harmonic 2-forms decompose as 11 from the $N_1$ building block and 10 from $N_2$,
consistent with the Mayer--Vietoris computation $b_2 = \rk(N_1) + \rk(N_2) = 11 + 10 = 21$.

\section{1-Form Hodge Laplacian and Spectral Democracy}

\subsection{1-Form Hodge Laplacian}

The Hodge Laplacian $\Delta_1 = d\delta + \delta d$ on 1-forms governs gauge boson masses. On a
Ricci-flat manifold, the Weitzenb\"ock identity gives $\Delta_1 = \nabla^*\nabla$ (the rough Laplacian),
since the Ricci curvature contribution vanishes.

\begin{theorem}
On $\Kseven$ with diagonal $s$-dependent metric, for a 1-form $\alpha = f(s)\, d\theta$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_1(f\, d\theta) = \Delta_0(f) \cdot d\theta
\end{equation}
That is, the 1-form Laplacian on transverse modes reduces exactly to the scalar
Laplacian.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $f$ depends only on $s$ and $\theta$ is a coordinate ($df/d\theta = 0$):
$\delta(f\, d\theta) = -{*}d{*}(f\, d\theta) = 0$ (the divergence vanishes);
$d(f\, d\theta) = f'\, ds \wedge d\theta$;
$\delta(f'\, ds \wedge d\theta) = -(1/\sqrt{g})(\sqrt{g} \cdot g^{ss} \cdot f')' = \Delta_0(f)$.
Therefore $\Delta_1(f\, d\theta) = (d\delta + \delta d)(f\, d\theta) = \Delta_0(f) \cdot d\theta$.
\end{proof}

The same argument applies to $f(s)\, d\psi$. The $d\theta$ and $d\psi$ channels therefore have
\textbf{identical eigenvalue spectra to the scalar Laplacian $\Delta_0$}.

\textbf{$ds$-channel.} For longitudinal 1-forms $\alpha = f(s)\, ds$, the operator takes a
different form: $\Delta_1(f\, ds) = -[(Pf)'/W]'\, ds$, where $P = \sqrt{g} \cdot g^{ss}$ and
$W = \sqrt{g}$. This leads to a Sturm--Liouville problem with modified coefficient
functions $p_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1/W$ and $w_{\mathrm{eff}} = g_{ss}/W$.

\textbf{Spectral democracy.} Despite the different operator, the $ds$-channel eigenvalues
are identical to the scalar eigenvalues to $10^{-4}$ precision, because
the fiber metric is flat in $s$ to $< 0.002\%$.

\textbf{$b_1 = 0$ confirmed.} No genuine zero modes appear in any 1-form channel. By the Seifert--van Kampen argument (\S2 of~[I]), $\pi_1(\Kseven) = \{1\}$, so $b_1 = 0$.

\section{Singular Limits and Spectral Stability}

\subsection{ADE Singularity Model}

The smooth $\Kseven$ produces only abelian gauge groups. To obtain the Standard Model
gauge group $\SU(3) \times \SU(2) \times \U(1)$, one must consider singular limits of the K3
fiber~\cite{ref1,ref11,ref12}. An orbifold singularity of type $A_{n-1}$ on K3 produces an $\SU(n)$
gauge group via the McKay correspondence.

We model singularities as localized metric perturbations --- bump functions centered
at position $s_0$ along the neck:
\begin{equation}
g_{K3}(s) \to g_{K3}(s) \times \big[1 + A \times \exp\!\big(-(s - s_0)^2/(2\sigma^2)\big)\big]
\end{equation}
with amplitude $A$ and width $\sigma$.

\section{Sturm--Liouville Solver Details}

\subsection{Finite Difference Discretization}

The Sturm--Liouville problem~\eqref{eq:SL}
is discretized on a uniform grid $s_j = s_{\min} + jh$, $j = 0, \ldots, N-1$, with
$h = (s_{\max} - s_{\min})/(N - 1)$. The stiffness matrix $A$ and mass matrix $B$ are
symmetric tridiagonal:
\begin{align}
A_{j,j} &= (p_{j+1/2} + p_{j-1/2})/h^2 + q_j \\
A_{j,j+1} = A_{j+1,j} &= -p_{j+1/2}/h^2 \\
B_{j,j} &= w_j
\end{align}
where $p_{j+1/2} = (p_j + p_{j+1})/2$ (arithmetic mean at half-grid points).

\subsection{Boundary Conditions}

\textbf{Dirichlet} ($e = 0$): rows 0 and $N-1$ are removed, yielding an $(N-2) \times (N-2)$ system.

\textbf{Neumann} ($de/ds = 0$): the full $N \times N$ system is used. Boundary rows are modified:
row~0 has $A_{0,0} = p_{1/2}/h^2 + q_0$ (only right-side flux);
row~$N-1$ has $A_{N-1,N-1} = p_{N-3/2}/h^2 + q_{N-1}$ (only left-side flux).

\subsection{Convergence Analysis}

Richardson extrapolation from 6 grid sizes ($N = 200$ to $1200$) yields the converged
spectral gap $\lambda_1 = 0.1244$ with uncertainty $\pm 0.0001$.

The dense solver \texttt{scipy.linalg.eigh} is used for Neumann problems. The sparse solver
\texttt{scipy.sparse.\allowbreak linalg.eigsh} with shift-invert ($\sigma = 0$) is used for Dirichlet
problems.

\section{$\PINN$ Training for K3 Harmonic Forms}

\subsection{Architecture}

Each harmonic 2-form $\omega_I$ is represented as a neural network mapping K3 coordinates
to 2-form components. The network has 4 hidden layers with 64 neurons each and
tanh activation. Training minimizes
\begin{equation}
L = \|\Delta_2 \omega_I\|^2 + \mu \times \sum_{J<I} |\langle\omega_I, \omega_J\rangle_{L^2}|^2
\end{equation}
where the first term enforces harmonicity and the second enforces $L^2$ orthogonality.

\subsection{Gram--Schmidt Orthonormalization}

After training all 20 forms, a Gram--Schmidt procedure is applied using the $L^2$
inner product to ensure $\langle\omega_I, \omega_J\rangle = \delta_{IJ}$.

\subsection{Intersection Form Validation}

The intersection form $Q_{IJ} = \int \omega_I \wedge {*}\omega_J$ is computed by numerical integration
over the K3 fiber. The signature $(1, 19)$ is consistent with the K3 lattice.
